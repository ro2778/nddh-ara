👶 Paediatric Sedative Premedication

Choose based on cooperation, previous experience, and clinical situation. Oral preparations may be mixed with a small volume of flavoured squash.

1st Line Options
Clonidine • Tasteless
4 mcg/kg oral (max 200 mcg)
Formulation: IV solution (150 mcg/ml) given orally, or 25/100 mcg tablets (can crush & disperse in water)
⏱ Onset: 45 mins
Dexmedetomidine (intranasal) • Tasteless
2-3 mcg/kg intranasal (max 200 mcg, round up to nearest 5 mcg)
100 mcg/ml solution via MAD device. Shorter acting than clonidine, minimal respiratory depression.
⏱ Onset: 25 mins
Dexmedetomidine (buccal)
1-4 mcg/kg buccal (max 200 mcg)
100 mcg/ml solution via MAD device
⏱ Onset: 25 mins
Dexmedetomidine (oral)
4 mcg/kg oral
100 mcg/ml solution given orally
⏱ Onset: 30-45 mins
Midazolam (oral/enteral) — Miprosed
0.25-0.5 mg/kg oral (max 20 mg)
Formulation varies — check concentration carefully
⏱ Onset: 30 mins
Midazolam (buccal) — Epistatus/Buccolam
300 mcg/kg buccal (max 10 mg)
Epistatus 10 mg/ml, Buccolam 5 mg/ml — CHECK concentration!
Age-based: 1-4y = 5 mg, 5-9y = 7.5 mg, 10-17y = 10 mg
⏱ Onset: 10-20 mins
2nd Line / Rescue
Ketamine (oral)
5-10 mg/kg oral (max 400 mg)
50 or 100 mg/ml solution given orally. Hallucinogenic — recover in quiet environment.
⏱ Onset: 20-30 mins
Ketamine + Midazolam combo (oral)
Ketamine 3 mg/kg + Midazolam as above
Over 1 year old only. Can be mixed together.
⏱ Onset: ~20 mins
Ketamine (IM) — Last Resort
5 mg/kg IM (max 400 mg)
Prepare in 5 ml syringe. Deltoid, vastus lateralis, or ventrogluteal. Discuss with parent/carer and document. Have parent hug/distract child.
⏱ Onset: 3-5 mins
Combinations: If single agent fails, combinations can be used (e.g. midazolam + clonidine). Use either clonidine OR dexmedetomidine, NOT both. If 2nd/3rd line needed, consider remaining with the child continuously. Dexmedetomidine can be mixed with midazolam.
Overweight children: Take care with dosing. Refer to SOBA guideline for dose adjustments based on BMI centiles.
Refused/vomited dose: Contact prescribing anaesthetist immediately. Do NOT transfer to theatre without discussing.

💨 Intranasal Administration (MAD Device)

• Use concentrated solution, max 0.3 ml per nostril ideal
• For 10-79 mcg: dilute to 0.8 ml total with NaCl 0.9%, give 0.4 ml per nostril
• For 80-200 mcg: use undiluted, divide between nostrils
• Use 2 ml syringe. Nestle sponge gently but firmly in nostril
• Add air bubble behind drug in syringe to clear dead space
• Short sharp push to atomise properly
• Check nostrils for mucus/blood first — suction if needed

🧑 Adult Sedative Premedication

Clear explanation and calm dialogue often suffices. If medication is indicated for anxiety, benzodiazepines are the mainstay.

1st Line — Anxiolysis
Lormetazepam • Preferred
1-2 mg oral (1 mg in elderly or <50 kg)
Tablets. Greater anxiolytic effect and less hangover than diazepam. Safe in renal impairment, caution in hepatic.
⏱ Onset: ~20 mins
Lorazepam
0.5-1 mg oral (lower dose in elderly/frail)
Tablets. Longer onset than lormetazepam, longer duration of action.
⏱ Onset: ~90 mins (much slower!)
Temazepam
10-30 mg oral (lower dose in elderly/frail)
Tablets
⏱ Onset: 1-2 hours
Diazepam
2.5-10 mg oral (lower dose in elderly/frail)
Tablets
⏱ Onset: 1-2 hours
2nd Line — Severe Agitation / Learning Difficulties
Midazolam (oral/buccal)
Oral: 0.25 mg/kg (max 20 mg) • Buccal: 0.3 mg/kg (max 10 mg)
CHECK formulation & concentration. Epistatus 10 mg/ml, Buccolam 5 mg/ml. Anaesthetist must be available to attend patient.
⏱ Onset: 20-30 mins
Clonidine
4 mcg/kg oral (max 200 mcg)
IV solution 150 mcg/ml given orally, or tablets 25/100 mcg
⏱ Onset: 45-60 mins
Dexmedetomidine (intranasal/buccal)
2-3 mcg/kg intranasal or 1-4 mcg/kg buccal (max 200 mcg)
100 mcg/ml solution. Outside of product license. Use MAD device.
⏱ Onset: 25 mins
Last Resort
Ketamine (oral or IM)
5 mg/kg oral or IM
Outside of license. Discuss with consultant. IM onset much faster (5 mins vs 20-30 mins oral).
⏱ Oral: 20-30 mins • IM: 5 mins
Midazolam for severe agitation: Pre-operative planning should have been made. Anaesthetist must be present on ward until transfer to theatre.

🧠 Patients with Learning Disabilities or Special Needs

These patients may require a tailored approach. Key principles:

Before the day:
• Northern Site: complete "Reasonable Adjustment Pathway"
• Eastern Site: use "This is me" passport
• Need for sedation should be identified at pre-assessment
• Consider non-pharmacological methods: Play Specialists, Learning Disability Liaison
Consent:
• If doubt about capacity → formal mental capacity assessment, document in notes
• If lacking capacity → best interests decision, least restrictive option
• Include the patient in discussions as much as their comprehension allows

Drug choices: Use the same agents as for paediatric or adult patients (select the appropriate tab). Key differences:

Lorazepam/Lormetazepam may be insufficient
In patients with severe agitation or learning difficulties, oral/buccal midazolam may be necessary. Anaesthetist must be available.
Up to 3 different medications may be needed
Under the direction of an anaesthetist. If 2nd/3rd line agents used, consider remaining with the patient continuously.
Complex cases — sedation at home or car park
Highly unusual. Requires extensive MDT planning BEFORE the day. Not covered by this guideline — discuss with consultant.

⚠️ Contraindications to Sedative Premedication

Careful risk assessment required if any of the following are present:
  • Difficult airway
  • Increased aspiration risk
  • Severe obstructive sleep apnoea
  • Raised intracranial pressure
  • Severe hepatic or renal impairment
  • Previous allergic or behavioural reaction to the planned premedication
  • New or unexplained reduced saturations on room air
  • Severe respiratory disease (e.g. COPD requiring long-term O₂)
  • Acute systemic illness (e.g. severe sepsis)
If sedation still warranted despite contraindications: discuss with a paediatric anaesthetist or on-call consultant first. The decision to give sedative premedication in these situations rests with the consultant.
Key Drug Interactions

• Use either clonidine OR dexmedetomidine — NOT both
• Midazolam: may see paradoxical agitation
• Ketamine: hallucinogenic — recover in quiet environment
• Overweight children: adjust doses per SOBA guideline

✅ After Administration

  • Patient should remain on ward under direct supervision of a responsible adult (parent/guardian)
  • When patient becomes drowsy, they should lie in bed or on a trolley
  • If they fall asleep → continuous SpO₂ monitoring with pulse oximetry
  • Oxygen should NOT be routinely applied
  • If premedication inadequate → additional medication at discretion of prescribing anaesthetist
  • If patient refuses, spits out or vomits medication → contact prescribing anaesthetist immediately. Do NOT transfer to theatre without discussing

👩‍⚕️ Roles

Anaesthetist: choice of sedative, correct prescription, ensure safe environment, inform parents/carers of effects

Nurse: check prescription, prepare and administer safely, communicate problems to anaesthetist, maintain safe environment with monitoring to avoid falls and unrecognised hypoxia
